What this document is — and why it exists

The audit deliverable has two halves: the written documentation (README + audit doc + optimization guide) and the live walkthrough. Documentation alone proves the work was described; the walkthrough proves the work actually runs. This document is the script for that walkthrough — designed so that anyone reading it understands not only what to click, but why each step was chosen and what the reviewer is meant to take away from seeing it.

Treat this as a presenter's full notes. Each section below contains four blocks:

  • Goal. What stakeholder takeaway this section produces.
  • Setup & actions. Numbered steps with copy-pasteable commands.
  • Reasoning. Why this step exists at all — the engineering principle behind it.
  • Narration. Verbatim text you can read on camera. It's written in spoken English, not bullet-point English.

If a reviewer pauses the recording and asks "why did you do it that way?", every answer is in this document.

01Format & production notes

Read once before you ever hit record.

Pre-record, do not present live

A live walkthrough is hostage to AWS console latency, expired tokens, and your own nerves. Pre-recording gives you the freedom to retake any section, edit out dead air, and zoom in on the moments that matter. Reviewers can scrub a recording at 1.25× speed; they cannot scrub a live call. Use OBS, Loom, or Windows Game Bar (Win+G) — whichever you already know. Quality bar:

  • 1080p+ video, mic audio (not just system audio) so your voice carries.
  • Zoom in on browser and terminal fonts so console text is legible on a phone screen.
  • Record in sections and edit. Do not attempt a single 25-minute take — you will fluff one line and want to rerecord the whole thing.
  • Pre-stage long Terraform applies off-camera. On-camera, run terraform output to show results without making reviewers watch a three-minute apply.
Why this matters

The single most common failure mode for walkthrough submissions is a 35-minute video where most of it is the presenter waiting for AWS. Reviewers don't grade you on "the apply succeeded"; they grade you on how clearly you explained why you built what you built. Make the recording about the explanation.

02The three "senior-engineer" moments

Three places to demonstrate judgement, not just button-clicking.

Most candidates can deploy a budget and an ASG. What separates a senior engineer from a tutorial-follower is being able to articulate why a particular configuration was chosen over its plausible alternatives. Three moments in this walkthrough are designed to surface that judgement. When you reach them, slow down — these are the sentences a reviewer will remember.

1
Safety triad (EBS GC, § 7)

Dry-run by default · tag-scope every destructive call · snapshot before delete. The script literally refuses to run if you combine --delete with an empty tag filter.

2
Layered alerts (Budget, § 8)

FORECASTED catches runaways before the dollars land; ACTUAL is the late-warning fallback. We layer both because forecasting can be wrong on small bases.

3
Allocation strategy (Spot ASG, § 10)

price-capacity-optimized balances current Spot price with pool depth — more interruption-resistant than the older lowest-price strategy.

Delivery tip

At each of these moments, pause for a beat and look at the camera. Don't power through them as if they were another checklist item. These are the sentences that prove you understand the why.

05Scenario framing

2 minutes · README on screen.

Goal of this section

Set the problem before showing the solution. Reviewers should know within the first minute (a) what scenario this audit is responding to, (b) what frameworks the work is anchored to, and (c) that every downstream claim has verified evidence behind it.

Step 1Open README § 1 — "The Problem"

Scroll to the section titled "The Problem" and read the inherited-account framing aloud. Do not summarize it — the framing has been written to sound natural read out. It establishes that this is a real-world scenario, not a contrived demo.

Step 2Scroll to § 2 — Executive Summary

Hover over the Controls table. Do not read every row. Instead, point at the "Verified by" column with your cursor and say one sentence: "every claim in this audit has an artifact behind it." That single move tells reviewers what they're about to watch.

Step 3Name the frameworks

Mention by name the two frameworks the audit is anchored to: the FinOps Foundation Framework (Inform / Optimize / Operate) and AWS Well-Architected — Cost Optimization Pillar. You are not going to teach either framework on camera; you are signalling that the work is auditable against external standards.

Why open this way

Reviewers form their assessment in the first ninety seconds. If you open by clicking around in the AWS console, they read it as a tutorial run-through. If you open by framing the problem, the rest of the recording is read as proof against that frame. Same content, different interpretation.

Narration — read aloud
"We inherited an AWS account with no cost controls, no tagging discipline, and no automated cleanup. The audit you're about to watch is the response. Three zombie classes detected, four controls deployed, one optimized architecture pattern — every claim is verified in eu-west-1 against account 648637468459. The work is anchored to the FinOps Foundation Framework and the AWS Well-Architected cost optimization pillar, so it maps cleanly against industry standards. Let's start with the findings."

06Inform — find the waste

4 minutes · EC2 console, Cost Explorer, CloudSweep Step Functions. CS + LAB

Goal of this section

Demonstrate that the three zombie classes were detected by multiple independent sources — the AWS console, AWS-native cost tooling, and the CloudSweep pipeline. Triangulation is what makes a finding credible. One source can be a glitch; three sources agreeing is a fact.

Step 1Show the Terraform outputs

The seed resources were created off-camera. Don't run terraform apply here — it adds three minutes for no benefit. Just print the outputs so reviewers see the three IDs the rest of the section will keep referring back to.

PowerShell
terraform -chdir=terraform/environments/dev output `
  | Select-String "lab_"

You should see lab_ebs_volume_id, lab_eip_allocation_id, and lab_idle_instance_id. Read the three IDs aloud once so reviewers can map them to what you show next.

Step 2Verify in the EC2 console — three tabs in sequence

EC2 → Volumes → filter tag:CostCenter = Lab → highlight the row with State available. Say: "available means no instance is attached — we're paying for storage that nothing reads or writes."

EC2 → Elastic IPs → filter the same tag → point at the empty Association column. Say: "AWS charges for unassociated EIPs at half a cent per hour — $3.65 a month for nothing."

EC2 → Instances → filter the same tag → click into the idle instance → Monitoring tab → point at CPUUtilization sitting near zero across the last seven days. Say: "This instance has been billed for seven days and done no work."

Step 3Verify in Cost Explorer

Cost Explorer → Service group-by → Daily granularity → 14-day window. The seed resources produce a small but visible spike. Point at it. If Trusted Advisor cost checks are available (Business / Enterprise Support only), show them here. If not, say so on camera — see the reasoning box below.

Step 4Verify via CloudSweep pipeline CS

Step Functions → cloudsweep-dev-sfn → most recent execution. The graph should show the full path: Scan → Evaluate → Decision → Remediate → NotifyComplete. Click the Scan state → Output tab. You will see two findings (EBS and EIP) as structured JSON. Call out that this is what a finding looks like programmatically — not a screenshot, a normalized data structure that downstream automation can act on.

Optionally open the cloudsweep-dev-findings DynamoDB table and show the same two findings persisted as rows. Skip if you're running tight on time.

Why three detection sources

Each source has a different blind spot. The console only shows what you remember to look at. Cost Explorer aggregates spend but doesn't identify which resource is wasteful. CloudSweep scans on a schedule and emits structured findings, but it currently has no EC2-idle scanner — which is why we needed CloudWatch metrics for that one instance. Triangulating across the three is what makes the audit defensible. It also surfaces the CloudSweep scope gap honestly, which is itself a sign of a trustworthy audit.

Narration — read aloud
"Three resources, three different detection paths, same conclusion. The console shows them, Cost Explorer shows the spend, and the CloudSweep pipeline emits structured findings that downstream automation can act on. The idle instance is detected via CloudWatch metrics rather than CloudSweep — that's a known scope gap, tracked as recommendation five in the prioritized plan. I'm flagging it now because an audit that hides its limitations isn't an audit, it's marketing."

07Automate — EBS garbage collector

3 minutes · Terminal. LAB · Judgement moment #1

Goal of this section

Demonstrate not just that the script works, but that it is safe to run unattended. Anyone can write a delete loop. The engineering value lies in the guards that prevent the loop from deleting the wrong thing.

Step 1Run a dry-run first

Notice we are not passing --delete. The script defaults to dry-run; this is the safe path. It enumerates candidates and prints the monthly $ estimate without touching anything.

PowerShell
python scripts/lab/garbage_collect_ebs.py `
  --region eu-west-1 --tag CostCenter=Lab --grace-days 0

The output ends with "DRY RUN — no action taken." Read that line out loud. It's the moment that tells reviewers the destructive path is opt-in, not opt-out.

Step 2Walk through the safety triad on screen

Print the script's help so the reviewer can see the contract:

PowerShell
python scripts/lab/garbage_collect_ebs.py --help

Point at three things:

  1. --delete is required for destructive action. Anything that mutates state should be opt-in.
  2. --tag KEY=VALUE is mandatory whenever --delete is set. The script refuses with exit code 2 if you combine --delete with an empty tag filter — that prevents "delete every EBS volume in the account" by accident.
  3. --snapshot-first produces a tagged recovery snapshot before any volume is deleted. The snapshot carries PreDeleteSnapshot=true and SourceVolumeId=<id> so it is itself discoverable.
Step 3Run the real delete with snapshot
PowerShell
python scripts/lab/garbage_collect_ebs.py `
  --region eu-west-1 --tag CostCenter=Lab --grace-days 0 `
  --delete --snapshot-first

The output shows two lines per volume: the snapshot creation, then the deletion. Switch to the EC2 console:

  • EC2 → Snapshots → filter tag:PreDeleteSnapshot = true → confirm the new snapshot is present.
  • EC2 → Volumes → refresh → confirm the lab volume is gone.
Judgement moment #1 — the safety triad

Pause for a beat here. Look at the camera. Say the next sentence slowly:

Narration — read aloud
"Three safety properties, none of which is optional. The script is dry-run by default — destructive action is opt-in. It refuses to run if you combine delete with an empty tag filter — that single guard prevents 'delete every EBS volume in the account' as a typo. And it snapshots before it deletes, with the snapshot tagged so it's discoverable. None of these are clever. All of them are the difference between a script you can leave running on a schedule and a script that ends a career."
Why this matters operationally

The cost optimization guide in this repo lists these as the "five rules of safe automated cleanup." The script demonstrates three of them in twenty lines of code. The remaining two — grace period (--grace-days N, default 7) and audit trail (structured log line per action) — are also implemented; you can mention them briefly if there is time.

08Govern — AWS Budget & SNS alerts

3 minutes · Budgets console + SNS + inbox. LAB · Judgement moment #2

Goal of this section

Demonstrate that monetary controls are wired end-to-end, not just configured. A budget without a confirmed downstream subscriber is a budget that fires into a void.

Step 1Show the budget configuration

Budgets console → cloudsweep-dev-lab-monthly-budget. Point at four properties in order: the $50 cap, the FORECASTED notification at 100 %, the ACTUAL notification at 80 %, and the SNS topic ARN on the destination side. Pause briefly on the FORECASTED + ACTUAL combination — that's the configuration choice you'll defend in a moment.

Step 2Show the SNS topic + confirmed subscription

SNS → topic cloudsweep-dev-lab-cost-alerts → Subscriptions tab. The email subscription should show status Confirmed. Say one sentence: "Unconfirmed subscriptions silently drop messages — confirming the email is not optional."

Step 3Prove end-to-end delivery with a test publish

The budget is configured, the subscription is confirmed — but is the SNS topic policy correct? A test publish takes three seconds and removes all ambiguity.

PowerShell
aws sns publish `
  --topic-arn arn:aws:sns:eu-west-1:648637468459:cloudsweep-dev-lab-cost-alerts `
  --subject "Walkthrough test" `
  --message "Budget alert path verified end-to-end." `
  --region eu-west-1

Switch to your inbox. The message should arrive within seconds. (Pre-stage a prior delivery in the inbox in case Outlook is slow.)

Judgement moment #2 — layered alerts
Narration — read aloud
"Two notifications, one budget. FORECASTED at a hundred percent catches runaways before the dollars land — AWS's projection at the five-to-seven-day mark of the month is usually within ten percent accuracy, so we get a real early-warning radar. ACTUAL at eighty percent is the late-warning fallback if forecasting underestimates, which it sometimes does on small bases. The combination is the cheapest insurance in cloud: one Terraform module, two notifications, every dollar of accidental runaway caught."
Why the topic policy matters

The single most common misconfiguration in this control is forgetting to grant budgets.amazonaws.com permission to publish to the SNS topic. The budget fires, sends to SNS, SNS refuses, and the alert never reaches the human — silently. The Terraform module wires this policy automatically; the test publish proves it is correct.

09Govern — CostCenter tag enforcement

3 minutes · IAM + terminal. LAB

Goal of this section

Demonstrate that the deny policy is not just deployed but actually denies. A policy that allows everything is just as broken as one that denies everything — the only way to know is to test both paths. This section is also the section reviewers tend to remember most viscerally, because watching a deny fire on screen is satisfying in a way watching a budget configuration is not.

Step 1Open the policy JSON

IAM → Policies → cloudsweep-dev-lab-require-costcenter → JSON tab. Point at the two Deny statements. The first denies ec2:RunInstances when aws:RequestTag/CostCenter is null. The second denies ec2:DeleteTags when the tag key is CostCenter. Say one sentence: "the second statement is what stops users from stripping the tag immediately after launch."

Step 2Assume the restricted role

Both tests must run as the role the policy is attached to, not as your admin user. Open a new terminal pane and assume the role:

PowerShell
$creds = aws sts assume-role `
  --role-arn arn:aws:iam::648637468459:role/cloudsweep-dev-lab-restricted-role `
  --role-session-name walkthrough `
  --query Credentials --output json | ConvertFrom-Json
$env:AWS_ACCESS_KEY_ID     = $creds.AccessKeyId
$env:AWS_SECRET_ACCESS_KEY = $creds.SecretAccessKey
$env:AWS_SESSION_TOKEN     = $creds.SessionToken
Step 3Negative test — launch without the tag

This call should fail with an explicit deny. If it succeeds, the policy is broken — abort the recording and fix it before continuing.

PowerShell
aws ec2 run-instances `
  --image-id ami-0c13c2049f369d641 --instance-type t3.micro `
  --count 1 --region eu-west-1

Expected response: UnauthorizedOperation — "explicit deny in an identity-based policy: cloudsweep-dev-lab-require-costcenter." Read the error message aloud. This is the moment the audit lands.

Step 4Positive test — launch with the tag

The same call, plus --tag-specifications, must succeed. Otherwise the policy denies legitimate work.

PowerShell
aws ec2 run-instances `
  --image-id ami-0c13c2049f369d641 --instance-type t3.micro `
  --count 1 --region eu-west-1 `
  --tag-specifications "ResourceType=instance,Tags=[{Key=CostCenter,Value=Lab}]"

You should see an InstanceId in the response. Terminate it immediately so you don't accumulate charges: 

aws ec2 terminate-instances --instance-ids <id> --region eu-west-1
Step 5Mention the enterprise variant

Briefly state, without scrolling away: "For multi-account organisations, the recommended pattern is an Organizations SCP plus a Tag Policy — that sits above IAM and can't be bypassed even by account-root. The SCP JSON is documented in docs/lab/tag-governance.md but isn't deployed here because the sandbox account is not part of an Organization."

Finally, reset your environment so subsequent commands run as your admin user again:

Remove-Item Env:AWS_ACCESS_KEY_ID, Env:AWS_SECRET_ACCESS_KEY, Env:AWS_SESSION_TOKEN
Narration — read aloud (after the negative test)
"Both paths tested. A deny policy that nobody ever exercises is theatre — the only way to know it works is to try to do the thing it's supposed to stop. The error message names the policy by name, which is exactly what you want for incident response: a developer who hits this in real life knows immediately what to ask their platform team for."
Why pair RunInstances deny with DeleteTags deny

Without the second statement, a developer could launch with the required tag and then immediately call ec2:DeleteTags to remove it. The policy would be technically present but operationally worthless. This is one of the most common tagging-policy bugs in production accounts.

10Optimize — Mixed Instances Spot ASG

4 minutes · ASG console + cost-comparison table. LAB · Judgement moment #3

Goal of this section

Demonstrate the textbook stateless-workload pattern — On-Demand baseline plus Spot scale-out — and articulate why each configuration choice was made. Anyone can deploy an ASG; the value lies in explaining why this ASG looks the way it does and not some other way.

Step 1Open the ASG configuration

EC2 → Auto Scaling Groups → cloudsweep-dev-lab-asg → Instance management tab. Pause on the Mixed Instances Policy block and read out the four knobs that matter:

  • On-Demand base capacity = 1. Service-level guarantee: at least one node is always present even if every Spot pool gets reclaimed.
  • On-Demand percentage above base = 0. Every node beyond the baseline is Spot — maximum savings.
  • Spot allocation strategy = price-capacity-optimized. AWS chooses pools that balance current price and pool depth. More interruption-resistant than the older lowest-price strategy.
  • Capacity rebalancing on. ASG preemptively replaces at-risk Spot instances before AWS reclaims them.

Switch to the Instances tab. The table shows the Lifecycle column: one row says On-Demand, the rest say Spot. The Availability Zone column shows three different AZs. Diversification across types and AZs is what makes Spot survivable.

Step 2Trigger a scale-out, if not already at desired = 4

If the ASG is at 2 instances, demonstrating the mix is more convincing at 4. Set the desired capacity to 4 and watch the events stream in.

PowerShell
aws autoscaling set-desired-capacity `
  --auto-scaling-group-name cloudsweep-dev-lab-asg `
  --desired-capacity 4 --region eu-west-1

Open the Activity tab and let reviewers watch the three Spot launches appear. Then return to the Instances tab and confirm the final mix: 1 On-Demand + 3 Spot, spread across AZs a, b, and c.

Step 3Show the cost math

Switch to README.md § 7.3. The table summarises a 4-node t3.micro fleet, 730 hours per month, eu-west-1 list prices:

ConfigurationHourlyMonthlyvs all On-Demand
All On-Demand$0.0456$33.29baseline
1 OD + 3 Spot (default)$0.0222$16.21−51 %
All Spot (no baseline)$0.0144$10.51−68 % · higher risk

Point at the middle row, then at the top row. The same ratio applied to a 100-node web tier saves $400+ per month for the cost of writing the module once.

Judgement moment #3 — allocation strategy
Narration — read aloud
"Four instance types across three availability zones gives EC2 twelve Spot pools to choose from — that's the diversification. The allocation strategy is price-capacity-optimized, which balances current Spot price against pool depth — more interruption-resistant than the older lowest-price strategy that just chases the cheapest pool until it runs out. The On-Demand baseline of one guarantees the service stays up even if every Spot pool gets reclaimed in the same five minutes. Net result: fifty-one percent off the steady-state bill for this workload, and the same ratio applies all the way up to a hundred-node fleet. One module, written once, multiplied across every team that adopts the pattern."
When NOT to use this pattern

Spot is the wrong choice for long-running stateful processes that cannot checkpoint, for workloads with per-instance licensing where the license cost dwarfs the Spot saving, and for strict SLAs without a fallback capacity plan. The full decision matrix is in the Cost Optimization Guide § 6.2. Don't sell Spot as universal — sell it as the right pattern for stateless tiers.

14Post-production checklist

After you stop recording, before you publish.

  1. Watch the recording end-to-end at 1.25× speed. Cut dead air — anything longer than 3 seconds of nothing. Reviewers will forgive a fluffed sentence; they will not forgive boredom.
  2. Confirm all on-screen text is legible. Zoom in on the smallest moment (probably the Step Functions JSON output) and check it would still be readable on a phone.
  3. Add a 5-second title card at the start: project name, your name, the date.
  4. Add a 5-second end card with the GitHub repo URL: github.com/celetrialprince166/finops-cost-detective.
  5. Upload as YouTube Unlisted (or Loom). Unlisted means anyone with the link can watch; it does not appear in search. That's the right balance for a portfolio piece.
  6. Update README.md § 12 — replace the "to be linked here after recording" placeholder with the YouTube or Loom URL. Commit with message docs: link walkthrough recording and push.
  7. Verify the link works from an incognito window. Unlisted videos still require the YouTube setting to be saved; double-check before sharing.
Final sanity check

Before you share the link, watch the first 90 seconds with the sound off. Does the screen layout communicate the scope of the work without your voice? If not, retake the framing section — that's the part reviewers form their assessment from.
